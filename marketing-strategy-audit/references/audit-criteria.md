# Audit criteria — deep definitions

Seven criteria for evaluating any marketing strategy document. Each criterion has: what it asks, what good looks like, what poor looks like, common failure patterns.

---

## 1. Specificity

**What it asks.** Are the claims grounded in concrete numbers (KPI targets, budget figures, dates, percentages, segment sizes, market shares), or stated in generic language?

**What good looks like.**
- "Reach 1.5M MAU by Q4 2026 in Almaty, up from 0 at launch (May 2026)"
- "Allocate $5.4M total — 35% brand, 25% performance, 14% influence + Telegram, 8% CRM"
- "Performance ROAS target 1.4× by Q4, accepting 0.8× in Q1 launch wave"

**What poor looks like.**
- "Grow brand awareness significantly"
- "Invest in performance channels"
- "Improve customer experience"

**Common failure patterns.**
- Strategic goals stated without numerical targets
- Budget mentioned only as "the marketing budget" without breakdown
- Timelines either missing entirely or vague ("by mid-2026")
- Numbers present but only for low-priority claims, never for the hero metrics

**How to score.**
- 80–100: every major claim carries specific numbers. Budget broken down by channel. KPIs have targets and deadlines.
- 50–79: most major claims have numbers, but some hero claims (e.g. "grow share") are generic.
- 20–49: a sprinkling of numbers but most strategic claims are vague.
- 0–19: essentially no concrete numbers anywhere. Reads as marketing copy, not strategy.

---

## 2. Verification

**What it asks.** Are the numbers backed by named sources (citations to research, regulatory data, internal trackers, study references), or do they float without provenance?

**What good looks like.**
- "Internet penetration 89% (DataReportal Digital 2026 Kazakhstan)"
- "Aided awareness 28% Q3 2025 (internal brand tracker, n=1,200)"
- "Competitor MAU 5.4M (SimilarWeb monthly visits, Sep 2025)"

**What poor looks like.**
- "Internet penetration is high" — no source, no number
- "Our brand is well-known" — no tracker reference
- "Competitor X has many users" — no scale fact

**Common failure patterns.**
- Numbers from the user's own analytics presented as fact without methodology
- Public-source numbers stated without naming the source
- "Industry estimates suggest..." (whose estimates?)
- Numbers from outdated sources without dating them

**How to score.**
- 80–100: every number carries a named source (T1/T2/T3 tier). Internal data attributed to specific trackers / dates.
- 50–79: most numbers cited, but a few hero stats float.
- 20–49: occasional citations but most numbers are uncited.
- 0–19: numbers everywhere, sources nowhere.

---

## 3. Risk register quality

**What it asks.** Is there a risk register? Are risks scattered (off-diagonal — low-prob/high-impact tail-risks AND high-prob/low-impact constant drags) or aligned on a single diagonal (all "medium-medium")?

**What good looks like.**
- 5–8 named risks with explicit probability + impact + owner + mitigation
- At least 1 tail-risk (low-prob, high-impact — regulatory action, black-swan competitor move)
- At least 1 constant drag (high-prob, low-impact — FX volatility, CPM creep)
- Mitigations stated as monitoring cadence + contingency move + trigger threshold

**What poor looks like.**
- No risk section at all
- Risks listed but all rated "medium probability, medium impact" (a flat diagonal — strategist hasn't separated likelihood from severity)
- Mitigations are aspirational ("we'll monitor closely") with no concrete trigger

**Common failure patterns.**
- All risks are about competitors (linearity bias — no supply / macro / regulatory risks named)
- Mitigations are restatements of the risk ("Risk: CPM inflation. Mitigation: manage CPM")
- Critical risks lack owners — no accountability path
- No quarterly review cadence on critical risks

**How to score.**
- 80–100: 5+ named risks with full probability × impact × owner × mitigation × trigger structure. At least 1 off-diagonal in each direction.
- 50–79: 3–5 risks named, mostly diagonal, mitigations present but vague.
- 20–49: 1–3 risks listed, no probability/impact scoring, mitigations missing.
- 0–19: no risk section.

## 4. Action specificity

**What it asks.** Does each initiative carry **owner + deadline + success metric**, or read as a wishlist?

**What good looks like.**
- "Q2 2026: Launch verified-seller program. Owner: VP Trust & Safety. Success: 60% of GMV through verified sellers by Q4."
- "Always-on creator program — 15 micro-influencers (>10K, <100K), monthly content. Owner: Brand Lead. Success: 12M cumulative reach + 4.5%+ engagement rate by year-end."

**What poor looks like.**
- "Invest in creator partnerships"
- "Launch loyalty program in 2026"
- "Improve performance media"

**Common failure patterns.**
- Initiative named but no owner — strategy reads as homework for an unnamed someone
- Deadlines vague ("in 2026", "next year")
- Success metrics missing — how would we know if it worked?
- All initiatives owned by "Marketing" (the department, not a role) — not actionable

**How to score.**
- 80–100: every initiative has owner + deadline + success metric + dependencies.
- 50–79: most initiatives have 2 of 3 (typically missing dependencies or success metric).
- 20–49: initiatives named but mostly missing owners or deadlines.
- 0–19: list of intentions, no operational detail.

---

## 5. Synthesis integrity

**What it asks.** Does the executive summary actually reflect the content of the appendix / detailed sections, or are they two disconnected documents?

**What good looks like.**
- Exec summary names 3 strategic bets, each traceable to a specific appendix section with detailed evidence
- Headline metrics in exec match KPI tree in business-goal section exactly
- Risk picture in exec aligns with risk register — same critical risks called out

**What poor looks like.**
- Exec talks about "premium positioning" but appendix research shows budget-conscious target consumers
- Exec promises 50% growth, KPI tree details only 20% growth bets
- Exec lists 5 priorities, appendix has 8 initiatives with no mapping to which serve which priority

**Common failure patterns.**
- Exec summary written first / by senior leadership, appendix written separately / by analysts — they were never reconciled
- Exec uses different metric vocabulary than appendix (e.g. "engagement" up top, "DAU" below)
- Exec promises things that have no supporting work in appendix
- Appendix has work that exec ignores

**How to score.**
- 80–100: exec is a faithful precis of appendix. Every exec claim has appendix support. Metric vocabulary consistent throughout.
- 50–79: exec mostly aligned but with 1–2 noticeable disconnects.
- 20–49: exec and appendix feel like different documents.
- 0–19: exec is marketing copy, appendix is unrelated analysis.

---

## 6. Reality check *(Full audit only, skipped in Quick score)*

**What it asks.** Does the document's claimed positioning, brand attribute strengths, and market understanding match observable reality?

**What good looks like.**
- Document claims "trusted modern banking" — App Store reviews back this up (4.6 stars, recent reviews praise trust and modernity)
- Document claims "the #2 in category" — SimilarWeb / Sensor Tower confirms #2 position
- Document claims "winning Gen Z" — Google Trends shows growing search interest among 18–24 demographic

**What poor looks like.**
- Document claims "premium experience" — App Store reviews average 2.3 stars, recent reviews complain about support and bugs
- Document claims "growing market share" — public press shows competitor outpacing
- Document claims "Gen Z brand" — actual installs / search interest skew 35+

**Common failure patterns.**
- Brand team's self-perception ahead of consumer reality
- Strategic claim not stress-tested against external signals
- Outdated picture of brand health (last refreshed 18+ months ago)

**How to score.** Compare each strategic claim to observable signals.
- 80–100: every major claim survives reality check
- 50–79: 1–2 noticeable gaps but overall picture holds
- 20–49: multiple claims contradicted by signals
- 0–19: document is in a different reality from observable signals

---

## 7. Completeness

**What it asks.** Are the standard blocks of a marketing strategy present, or are there gaping holes?

**Reference set — what a complete strategy covers:**

- Market context (size, growth, dynamics)
- Consumer / segments
- Competitive set (direct + indirect)
- Positioning / brand
- KPI tree (targets per funnel stage)
- Media mix / channel strategy
- Budget allocation
- Risk register
- Next steps / governance

**What good looks like.** All 9 blocks present and substantive. Section sizes proportional to importance.

**What poor looks like.**
- "Strategy" turns out to be just a positioning statement + 3 channel tactics, no consumer / competitive / KPI / budget / risk work
- Several blocks are 1-line placeholders ("budget: TBD")

**Common failure patterns.**
- Heavy positioning + creative section, missing KPI / budget / risk
- Heavy KPI / numbers section, missing positioning / consumer
- All sections present but 7 are 1-paragraph each — completeness through breadth, no depth anywhere

**How to score.**
- 80–100: all 9 blocks present and at least 200 words each (or visual equivalent in slide-deck format)
- 50–79: 7–8 of 9 present, 1–2 weak
- 20–49: 4–6 of 9 present
- 0–19: under 4 blocks present — this isn't a strategy, it's a fragment
